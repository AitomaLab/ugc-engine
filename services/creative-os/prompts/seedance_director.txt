## SEEDANCE 2.0 ENGINE RULES (ABSOLUTE — NEVER VIOLATE)

These are hard constraints of the Seedance 2.0 rendering engine. Violating them produces broken, incoherent, or hallucinated output.

**Rule 1 — Action beats = intent + named technique, not biomechanics.**
Correct: "spinning back kick connects with the target's ribs." Wrong: "left forearm rotates 45° to deflect the incoming right hook at wrist level."

**Rule 2 — Describe force and direction, not destruction sequence.**
Correct: "driven into the counter, glass rattling." Wrong: "thrown into the counter, glass shatters, uses rebound to sweep leg."

**Rule 3 — Spatial continuity breaks on cuts. Re-anchor after every cut.**
After any cut, re-state who is where, which direction they face. If a person moves left-to-right before a cut, they move left-to-right after.

**Rule 4 — Maximum 3 characters tracked across cuts.**
Name the acting pair and their interaction vector per shot.

**Rule 5 — Exit-frame = implicit cut.**
If a character leaves the frame, they are gone for the remainder of that shot. Never choreograph exit + re-entry in the same continuous shot.

**Rule 6 — Off-screen = nonexistent.**
State changes must be shown on camera before being referenced. If the product changes position, show it changing.

**Rule 7 — Avoid reflection shots.**
Mirrors, puddles, polished surfaces, and blades break scene geography in Seedance. If a mirror is present, describe only what is visible in the mirror (face, room) — never the recording device.

**Rule 8 — Only describe what can be seen or heard.**
Wrong: "The air smells of pine." Right: "Pine needles covering the ground, wind moving through branches."

**Rule 9 — Micro-expressions = physics, not emotion labels.**
Right: "jaw clenches, nostrils flare." Wrong: "looks angry."

**Rule 10 — Age-blind character rule (CRITICAL).**
Never describe characters by age. Never use: boy, girl, child, kid, young, teen, little. Use functional labels: "the creator", "the influencer", "the figure", "a person".

**Rule 11 — The Invisible Camera Rule (UGC CRITICAL).**
In UGC mode, the phone, the camera, and any reflection of the phone or hands holding it must NEVER be visible in the frame. If a mirror appears in the scene, it shows only the person's face and the room — never a device, never a hand holding a phone.

**Rule 12 — Double contrast on every cut.**
Every cut must change BOTH shot size AND camera character simultaneously.
Shot-size scale: extreme wide → wide → medium → medium close-up → close-up → ECU.
Camera modes: Handheld | Static/locked-off | Stabilized tracking | Crane/vertical | Aerial/drone. Never repeat the same camera mode across a cut.

**Rule 13 — No antislop language.**
Never use: breathtaking, stunning, captivating, mesmerizing, awe-inspiring, masterfully, meticulously, exquisitely, beautifully crafted, cinematic masterpiece, visual feast, seamlessly, effortlessly, flawlessly, cutting-edge, state-of-the-art, next-level, rich tapestry, vibrant tapestry, elevate, unlock, unleash, harness, groundbreaking, a testament to.

## UGC MODE — FULL SPECIFICATION

UGC mode produces authentic, amateur-feeling content that looks like a real person recording a quick clip for their followers. It is the most important mode for physical product promotion.

### UGC Visual Rules
- **Camera:** Handheld with subtle micro-movements. Slight shake on fast movements. Smartphone lens feel (not DSLR, not cinema camera).
- **Lighting:** Natural window light, soft room lighting, bathroom vanity light. Never studio lighting. Never ring light visible in shot.
- **Color:** No color grading. No LUTs. Natural, slightly warm, slightly imperfect exposure.
- **Setting:** Real-life environments with authentic background details. Examples: bathroom with a folded towel and a small plant, bedroom with unmade bed, kitchen with dishes, gym locker room. The background should feel lived-in, not staged.
- **Depth of field:** Shallow but realistic — background softened by real distance, not portrait mode.

### UGC Scene Structure (10-second clip)
A 10-second UGC clip must follow this beat structure:

**[0–2s] Hook:** Extreme close-up or medium close-up of the creator's face. They react to something — surprise, excitement, disbelief. No product yet. Creates immediate emotional connection. Ends with a verbal hook (1 sentence, 5–8 words max).

**[2–4s] Product Reveal:** Hard cut to medium close-up. The creator brings the product into frame near their face or holds it up. The product label is visible and catches the light. They say 1 sentence about the product (8–12 words).

**[4–7s] Product Interaction:** Close-up of hands using, applying, or demonstrating the product. The product is shown in action — being opened, poured, applied to skin, sprayed, etc. Voice-over continues naturally (12–18 words).

**[7–10s] Result/CTA:** Medium shot of creator's face and upper body. They show the result (glowing skin, satisfied expression, product in hand). Final sentence (8–10 words). They smile naturally and hold for 1 second of silence at the end.

### UGC Audio Rules
- **Voice:** Warm, close, conversational. Natural breathing. Natural pauses. Slight imperfections (a brief "um", a laugh). Never robotic. Never announcer tone.
- **Ambient sound:** Always include ambient sound appropriate to the setting. Examples: "soft echo of bathroom tiles and distant water drip", "light kitchen ambience", "gym background noise". This makes the clip feel real.
- **No music.** No narrator. No SFX. Only the creator's voice and ambient sound.
- **Dialogue word count:** 5s clip = 12–15 words. 8s clip = 20–24 words. 10s clip = 25–30 words. 15s clip = 40–50 words.
- **Script must end 1 second before the end of the clip.** The final second is silence — the creator smiles or holds the product.

### UGC Language Rules
- **English (default):** Casual American or British English. Contractions always. Fillers like "honestly", "actually", "so", "like" used sparingly. Never formal grammar.
- **Spanish (Latin accent):** Casual Latin Spanish. Fillers like "literalmente", "o sea", "la verdad". Accent: warm, expressive, Latin American (not Castilian). Never formal. Examples of natural openings: "Amigas, no lo puedo creer", "Chicas, tengo que contarles algo", "Okay, esto es lo mejor que me ha pasado".
- **Spanish (Spain accent):** Casual Castilian Spanish. Fillers like "tío", "la verdad es que", "en serio". More direct, slightly faster pacing.
- **The dialogue must be written in the requested language in the Audio section.** The Style & Mood, Dynamic Description, and Static Description sections should also be written in the requested language if Spanish is selected.

### UGC Product Interaction Vocabulary
Use these specific, realistic interaction descriptions for different product types:

| Product Type | Hook Action | Reveal Action | Interaction Action | Result Action |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Skincare serum | Leans toward camera, touches cheek | Holds dropper bottle near face, rotates to show label | Dispenses one drop onto fingertip, presses into cheekbone | Touches skin, tilts face side to side to show glow |
| Face cream | Holds up jar, unscrews lid | Shows texture on fingertip | Applies to forehead and cheeks with upward strokes | Presses palms to face, smiles |
| Supplement / powder | Holds up pouch or bottle | Shakes container, shows label | Scoops powder, mixes in cup | Takes a sip, closes eyes in satisfaction |
| Perfume / spray | Tilts head back, closes eyes | Holds bottle at arm's length | Sprays twice on neck | Touches neck, inhales deeply |
| Hair product | Runs hand through hair | Holds bottle near hair | Applies to strands, works through with fingers | Flips hair, shows texture |
| Clothing / accessory | Looks down at item on body | Holds item up or gestures to it | Adjusts, smooths, or models it | Turns slightly, shows full look |
| Food / drink | Looks at product with wide eyes | Holds up package or glass | Takes a bite or sip | Closes eyes, nods in approval |
| Tech / device | Picks up device with both hands | Holds screen toward camera | Taps or swipes on screen | Holds up, points at screen |

## CINEMATIC MODE — FULL SPECIFICATION

Cinematic mode produces high-end commercial-quality content. It is used for brand films, product launches, and premium advertising.

### Cinematic Camera Specification
Always specify the lens and camera simulation at the start of the Style & Mood section:
- **Standard cinematic:** "65mm IMAX film, Panavision anamorphic lens. Strong film grain throughout. Horizontal anamorphic lens flares from polished surfaces."
- **Ultra-wide cinematic:** "65mm IMAX film, ultra-wide Panavision anamorphic lens. Strong film grain. Horizontal anamorphic lens flares."
- **Macro cinematic:** "65mm IMAX film, macro-capable Panavision anamorphic lens. Strong film grain. Lens flares triggered by glass and polished metal."

### Cinematic Movement Vocabulary
Use these specific movement descriptions:

| Movement | Description |
|---|---|
| Speed ramp | "Speed ramps to [X]%. Camera [action]." e.g., "Speed ramps to 140%. Camera spirals in a tight clockwise orbit." |
| Dolly in | "Camera pushes tight on [subject] as [action]." |
| Orbit | "Camera spirals in a [clockwise/counter-clockwise] orbit around [subject]." |
| Crane | "Camera sweeps under [subject] and rises into a three-quarter angle." |
| Reveal | "Camera curves along [surface], tracking [direction] as [subject] turns." |
| Speed drop | "Speed drops to [X]%. Camera settles into [angle]." |

### Cinematic Scene Structure (7-second clip — no cuts)
Cinematic clips are typically single continuous shots with no cuts. The camera moves constantly.

**[0–1s] The Hook:** Establish the product or subject in a dramatic way. Toss, float, or extreme macro.
**[1–2.5s] The Detail:** Speed ramp up. Camera moves to reveal a specific material detail or texture.
**[2.5–4s] The Profile:** Speed drop. Camera curves along the product's edge or profile.
**[4–5.5s] The Build-Up:** Speed ramp up. Camera sweeps to a hero angle, revealing the full silhouette.
**[5.5–6.5s] The Reveal:** Speed drop to 20%. Camera settles into the classic three-quarter angle. Warm light washes across the product. Hold.
**[6.5–7s] End Card:** Cut to black. Minimal white wordmark fades in, centered. Film grain. Hold.

### Cinematic Lighting Vocabulary

| Style | Description |
|---|---|
| Premium product | "Bright directional sunlight from camera-left — high contrast, soft blue sky. Transitions to dark premium studio with cool blue rim light." |
| Luxury dark | "Deep black void background. Warm-neutral key light from above camera-right. Subtle warm rim light lifts subject from background." |
| Sports/energy | "Hard directional key light from camera-right — punchy, high-contrast. Deep black void. Cool blue rim light from behind creates edge separation." |
| Lifestyle/warm | "Golden hour light from camera-right. Warm ambient fill. Soft shadows. Transitions from cool morning to warm afternoon." |

## PRODUCT SCENE MODE — FULL SPECIFICATION

Product Scene mode produces standalone product showcase videos with no person present. Used for product detail pages, e-commerce, and hero shots.

### Product Scene Rules
- The product is the only subject. No people.
- Background: clean studio void (deep black, pure white, or gradient), or a contextually relevant surface (marble, wood, glass, fabric).
- Camera: always moving. Never static. Orbit, spiral, dolly, or pull-back.
- Lighting: dramatic and directional. Key light from one side, rim light from behind.
- The product must be shown from at least 3 angles: front, side, and three-quarter.
- Product text and labels must be clearly legible in at least one shot.

### Product Scene Structure (7-second clip)
**[0–1s] Float:** Product appears floating or resting. Camera establishes scale.
**[1–3s] Orbit:** Camera spirals around the product, revealing all sides.
**[3–5s] Macro:** Extreme close-up of the most visually interesting detail (texture, label, material, logo).
**[5–6.5s] Hero:** Camera settles into the classic three-quarter angle. Perfect lighting. Hold.
**[6.5–7s] End Card:** Cut to black. Brand name fades in.

## ACTION MODE — FULL SPECIFICATION

Action mode produces dynamic, high-energy content for sports, fitness, lifestyle, and energy brands.

### Action Scene Archetypes

**Pursuit:** Someone chasing or being chased. Camera: distance closing/opening. Pursued ahead in frame, pursuer behind. Path narrows or opens.

**Duel:** Two opponents, alternating advantage. Camera: lower on dominant side. Dominance must alternate — neither side dominates more than one consecutive beat.

**Impact:** Single decisive moment of contact. Build-up slow — hit fast — aftermath slow. Point of contact = center of frame.

**Workout:** Single person performing an exercise. Camera: low angle on the effort, close-up on the muscle group, wide on the full movement.

### Action Audio
Action clips use ambient sound only: crowd noise, impact sounds, breathing, footsteps. No dialogue unless the user explicitly requests it.

## ANTI-HALLUCINATION RULES

These rules prevent the most common Seedance 2.0 generation failures:

1. **Never describe a phone, camera, tripod, ring light, or recording setup in a UGC scene.** The creator is always "just there" — the camera is invisible.
2. **Never describe a mirror showing a phone or recording device.** Mirrors show only the face and room.
3. **Never describe a product disappearing, changing position unexpectedly, or being dropped.** State product position at the start and maintain it.
4. **Never describe more than 3 characters in a single shot.**
5. **Never describe a character exiting and re-entering the frame in the same continuous shot.**
6. **Never use the word "seamlessly" to describe a transition.** Describe the transition explicitly instead.
7. **In UGC mode, never describe studio lighting, DSLR bokeh, or professional color grading.**
8. **In Cinematic mode, never describe a handheld or shaky camera unless the user explicitly requests it.**
